EC 1.7.2.3
trimethylamine-N-oxide reductase (cytochrome c)

Abstract:
Trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) has been found in a variety of marine organisms including fish, molluscs, crustaceans and seaweed [1]. TMAO is found in marine organisms as an osmolyte in combination with urea. In sharks, who lack a swim bladder, TMAO and urea contribute to buoyancy. A number of marine-fish spoilage organisms use TMAO as an electron acceptor in anaerobic respiration [2]:
trimethylamine N-oxide + 2 H+ + 2 e− → trimethylamine + H2O
(note the reaction proceeds in a direction opposite to the one chosen in EC 1.7.2.3). Trimethylamine (TMA), the volatile product of this reaction, has the specific odour of rotting fish. In Escherichia coli, the TMAO respiratory chain consists of a quinol electron donor, a membrane-bound multiheme c-type cytochrome TorC, and a periplasmic terminal reductase TorA that contains bis(molybdopterin guanine dinucleotide)molybdenum cofactor. This molybdoenzyme belongs to the dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) reductase family. Recently, a family of molecular chaperones responsible for the molybdopterin cofactor incorporation in TMAO reductase and other homologous enzymes has been recognised. TorD protein in Escherichia coli is a chaperone that binds to the unfolded TorA protein before insertion of the molybdopterin cofactor. The crystal structure of TMAO reductase from Shewanella massilia [3], as well as of the DMSO reductases from Rhodobacter sphaeroides [4] and Rhodobacter capsulatus [5], have been solved. All the proteins are folded into four α/β domains. A large funnel-like cavity penetrates into the protein and leads to the molybdenum atom, which is located approximately in the centre of the molecule.
