EC 3.5.1.5
Urease

Abstract:
Urea is highly stable in aqueous solutions ranging in pH from 2 to 12, with a half-life of 3.6 years at 38 °C [1]. The non-enzymatic degradation of urea in aqueous solution proceeds via elimination, yielding ammonia and isocyanic acid as sole products:

urea → HNCO + NH3

In contrast, urease carries out a true hydrolysis of urea to produce ammonia and carbamic acid, which is further decomposed into ammonia and CO2 [2]:

urea + H2O → carbamic acid + NH3
carbamic acid → CO2 + NH3

The kcat/KM of urease is 1014-fold higher than the rate of the uncatalysed elimination reaction, implying the "proficiency" of urease is >1014, because the uncatalysed hydrolysis reaction must be even slower (in fact it was never observed) [1].
The crystallisation of jack bean urease by James Sumner in 1926 provided firm proof of the protein nature of enzymes and brought him the Nobel Prize in Chemistry 20 years later "for his discovery that enzymes can be crystallized". However, it was not until 1975 that it was revealed that the active site of this enzyme contains two nickel atoms [2]. One unusual feature is that the metal atoms are bridged by an N6-carboxy-L-lysine residue. Several isoforms of urease such as canatoxin display insecticidal effects that are independent of ureolytic activity and require proteolytic processing of the protein by insect enzymes. It was hypothesised that the plant ureases are protoxins, and can be considered to be the plant arsenal of defenses against insects [3].
