EC 4.1.99.3
Deoxyribodipyrimidine photo-lyase

Abstract:
Ultraviolet sunlight damages genomic DNA by forming lesions, the major chemical form of which is a cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer (CPD) formed by the cycloaddition of two adjacent pyrimidine rings. Deoxyribodipyrimidine photolyase (EC 4.1.99.3) repairs the CPD of DNA and has been found in various organisms. The enzyme utilizes visible light as an energy source to cleave the cyclobutane linkage. Photolyases are known to have two chromophores with independent roles to harvest the light energy and to catalyse the reaction. FAD is found in all known photolyases as a catalytic cofactor and transfers an electron to CPD. Two prosthetic groups are well known as light-harvesting cofactors. One is 5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate, which is found in the photolyase from Escherichia coli and the other is 8-hydroxy-5-deazaflavin, which is found in the photolyase from Anacystis nidulans. Recently, FMN was found as a light-harvesting cofactor in photolyase from Thermus thermophilus [1]. The first crystal structure of an archaeal photolyase from an acidothermophilic archaeon, Sulfolobus tokodaii (St-photolyase), has been reported at 2.8 Å resolution [PDB:2E0I]. Unexpectedly, an FAD molecule was identified at the position of the light-harvesting cofactor of St-photolyase [2]. This cofactor appears to be incorporated during the expression of the recombinant St-photolyase in E. coli. This second FAD molecule is well accommodated within the crystal structure, suggesting that FAD works as a novel light-harvesting cofactor of photolyase.
