EC 2.7.1.11
Phosphofructokinase

Abstract:
Phosphofructokinase (PFK) catalyses the phosphorylation of fructose 6-phosphate to fructose 1,6-bisphosphate. Mammals, yeast and many protozoa and bacteria use ATP as the phosphono donor (EC 2.7.1.11):

ATP + D-fructose 6-phosphate = ADP + D-fructose 1,6-bisphosphate

whereas plants and certain other protozoa and bacteria possess PFKs that use inorganic diphosphate (PPi) (EC 2.7.1.90):

diphosphate + D-fructose 6-phosphate = phosphate + D-fructose 1,6-bisphosphate

In many cases, both types of enzyme are present in the same organism. The two PFK families share a distant common ancestor, and typically have about 20-25% sequence identity. Interestingly, trypanosomal ATP-dependent PFK belongs to the PPi-dependent PFK family by sequence similarity.
The crystal structures of the bacterial PFKs have been studied in detail [1]. Martinez-Oyanedel et al. recently reported the first crystal structure of a eukaryotic PFK [2]. The structure of the ATP-dependent PFK from Trypanosoma brucei revealed an N-terminal domain which possesses a unique fold. In addition, the loop that is characteristic for trypanosomal PFKs (it is not present in the bacterial or human enzymes) has been found to be located immediately adjacent to the active site. This knowledge offers opportunities for structure-based drug discovery for the treatment of sleeping sickness and other diseases caused by the trypanosomatids.
