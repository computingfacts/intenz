EC 1.2.1.70
Glutamyl-tRNA reductase

Abstract:
δ-Amino levulinic acid (ALA) is the general precursor molecule for the biosynthesis of tetrapyrroles like chlorophylls, hemes, and coenzyme B12. Plants, archaea, and the majority of bacteria form ALA in a two-step reaction from the C5-skeleton of L-glutamate bound to tRNAGlu (glutamyl-tRNA). The initial enzyme of the pathway glutamyl-tRNA reductase (EC 1.2.1.70), encoded by hemA, reduces the activated α-carboxyl group of glutamate using NADPH to form glutamate 1-semialdehyde (GSA). GSA is then converted to ALA by a second enzyme glutamate-1-semialdehyde 2,1-aminomutase (GSAM; EC 5.4.3.8) [1]. The glutamyl-tRNA reductase from bacteria and plants contains one tightly bound heme per protein subunit, while a recent structure from the archae Methanopyrus kandleri did not reveal any cofactor for this organism [2]. Instead the structure revealed that glutamyl-tRNA reductase is found as a dimer with a very extended V-shape. Experiments with the Escherichia coli enzyme shows that this structure allows an intimate interaction with the second enzyme in the pathway EC 5.4.3.8, thus allowing the tunneling of the unstable GSA between the two enzymes [3].
