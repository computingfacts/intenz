EC 4.2.1.84
Nitrile hydratase

Abstract:
Nitrile hydratases (NHases) are mononuclear iron or (non-corrinoid) cobalt enzymes that catalyse the hydration of a large number of diverse nitriles to their corresponding amides:
R-C≡N + H2O → R-C(O)NH2
Organisms expressing NHases are capable of utilising aliphatic nitriles as the sole source of nitrogen. NHases have been efficiently used for the industrial production of acrylamide from acrylonitrile and for the removal of nitriles from wastewater.
Photosensitive NHases intrinsically possess nitric oxide (NO) bound to the metal centre and its photodissociation activates the enzyme. NHases are composed of two types of subunits, α and β, which have unrelated amino acid sequences. NHases exist as αβ dimers or α2β2 tetramers and bind one metal atom per αβ unit. The protein ligands to the metal are the sidechains of the three cysteine residues and two mainchain amide nitrogens. The two Cys residues coordinated to the metal are post-translationally modified to Cys-sulfinic (Cys-SO2H) and -sulfenic (Cys-SOH) acids. NHases have been extensively studied and a number of crystal structures of both iron- and cobalt-dependent NHases have been solved. Even so, the catalytic mechanism of NHase, the role of the metal centre in catalysis, and the molecular basis of the substrate selectivity remain unclear.
